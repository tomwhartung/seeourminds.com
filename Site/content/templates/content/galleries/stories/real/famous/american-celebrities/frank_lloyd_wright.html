In 1991 the American Institute of Architects recognized Frank Lloyd Wright as the greatest American architect of all time — 32 years after his death in 1959. Although he never joined their organization, Mr. Wright certainly agreed with them, and did not think it is arrogant for him to say so.

I've been accused of saying I was the greatest architect in the world, and if I had said so, I don't think it would be very arrogant, because I donâ€™t believe there are many [great architects] — if any. For 500 years what we call architecture has been phony.
 — Frank Lloyd Wright, in an interview with Mike Wallace, September, 1957.

The distinctive beauty of some of his greatest works — the Johnson Wax Headquarters, Fallingwater, and the Guggenheim Museum, for example — speaks for itself. And the drama he experienced with his wives helps make his life an interesting, albeit tragic, story.

Architects Need Logic ...

The Green in Frank Lloyd Wright's spiritual portrait represents his preference for being logical rather than emotional. His preference is very slight, so his portrait also contains quite a bit of Red, representing his ability to make emotional decisions when appropriate.

Architecture is obviously an occupation in which its practitioners need to be logical. They must know how to use math and geometry to design their structures using precise specifications that everyone building the edifice can understand and use to get their job done.

... And Feeling, Too?

Frank Lloyd Wright's buildings are bold and distinctive, and it can seem like they have feeling. Indeed, in an article in the May 1914 issue of the Architectural Record he states how, during his apprenticship at the firm of Adler and Sullivan, he learned to use the opposing processes of thinking and feeling to create a sentient, rational building:

This ideal combination of Adler and Sullivan was then working to produce what no other combination of achitects nor any individual architect at that time dared even preach: a sentient, rational building that would owe its style to the integrity with which it was individually fashioned to serve its particular purpose; a thinking as well as a feeling process, requiring the independent work of true artist imagination.
 — Frank Lloyd Wright in In the Cause of Architecture II,[2] 1914. [Emphasis added.]

Interestingly, Wright confessed to feeling more emotion for some of his buildings than for his children:

I have had the father feeling for a building, but I never had it for my children.
 — Frank Lloyd Wright.[1]

As far as Mr. Wright's morality and family life goes, he felt he was above all that. Ultimately, the validity of this claim is up to the ethics and conscience of the beholder.

Expressing Ideas

The Blue in Frank Lloyd Wright's spiritual portrait represents his preference for ideas over facts. His definition of architecture in the May, 1930 issue of the Architectural Forum as the scientific art of making structure express ideas[2] confirms how he considered ideas to be the central elements of his work.

Mr. Wright began his career in Chicago in 1888 as an apprentice working for Louis Sullivan, who is famous for espousing the idea that form follows function. At first Wright embraced his boss's idea, but years later, it was clear he had moved beyond such obvious observations:

Form follows function is mere dogma until you realize the higher truth that form and function are one.
 — Frank Lloyd Wright, from the Architect's Journal, August, 1936.[2]

Organic Architecture

Wright took the idea of form follows function and developed it into an entire philosophy he called organic architecture. In his book The Disappearing City, which outlines his plan for the development of his concept for the ultimate suburbia he called Broadacre City, he goes so far as to say:

I now realize that organic architecture is life and life itself is organic architecture or both are in vain.
 — Frank Lloyd Wright,[2] 1932.

Regardless of how his critics might feel about Frank Lloyd Wright and his work, one has to admire a person who approaches their life's work with such dedication and sincerity. Wright's staunch forthrightness explains how his competitors and critics could admire and praise him, even while disliking and criticizing him.

Some of Mr. Wright's Ideas

What makes Frank Lloyd Wright's work so different from that of other architects is the insight inherent in his uniquely American ideas about architecture — and the way he expresses these insightful ideas through his designs.

Wright put forth some of his key ideas in the March 1908 issue of the Architectual Record.

A building should appear to grow easily from its site and be shaped to harmonize with its surroundings of nature if manifest there, and if not try to make it as quiet, substantial, and organic as she would have been were the opportunity hers.
We of the Middle West are living on the prairie. The prairie has a beauty of its own and we should recognize and accentuate this natural beauty, its quiet level. Hence, gently sloping roofs, low proportions, quiet sky lines, suppressed heavy-set chimneys, and sheltering overhangs, low terraces and out-reaching walls sequestering private gardens.
 — Frank Lloyd Wright in In the Cause of Architecture, I[2], 1908.

These ideas exemplify the goals underlying the Prairie School of Architecture.

Profound and Profounder

These ideas are certainly profound, but they seem trite and superficial when compared to Frank Lloyd Wright's deeper, more general aesthetic goals:

Nothing is more difficult to achieve than the integral simplicity of organic nature, amid the tangled confusions of the innumerable relics of form that encumber life for us. To achieve it in any degree means a serious devotion to the underneath in an attempt to grasp the nature of building a beautiful building beautifully, as organically true in itself, to itself and to its purpose, as any tree and flower.
 — Frank Lloyd Wright in In the Cause of Architecture II,[2] 1914. [Emphasis in original.]

It's easy to see how a skeptic could read this quote once and conclude Mr. Wright has a screw loose. Perhaps the skeptic could be seduced into appreciation by a short video tour of Fallingwater, followed by a re-reading — or maybe two or three re-readings — of the above quote.

One of the best ways to explain an idea is often with a good example.

The Architect Archetype: INTX?

Fans of Jungian archetypes and the MBTI® may be familiar with the Architect archetype. A search of the internet quickly yields two common examples:

  • The Architect role variant developed by David Keirsey, which identifies architects as representive of the INTP four-letter type.
  • The site 16personalities.com refers to the INTJ personality type as The Architect.

As an ENTJ, Frank Lloyd Wright's personality was certainly close enough to the ideal architect.

Mr. Wright's personality was definitely on the decisive side. In 1908 he made it plain that the best buildings were a gestalt, in which furniture and color schemes form their respective parts of the whole.

The most truly satisfactory apartments are those in which most or all of the furniture is built in as a part of the original scheme. The whole must always be considered as an integral unit.
 — Frank Lloyd Wright in In the Cause of Architecture, I[2], 1908.

Wright often designed the stained glass windows and furniture for his houses, and once reportedly even designed the hostess' dress. There is even a story about a client who moved their furniture in an attempt to make the space their own, only to have Mr. Wright pay an impromptu visit and move the furniture back to where he thought it should be.[3]

The Celebrity Architect

More significantly, in contrast to the introversion present in the ideal architect, Mr. Wright was certainly extroverted. Born in 1867, just two years after the Civil War, it's incredible to realize he appeared on television when it became popular in the 1950s — nearly a century later.

The crowd's reaction to Frank Lloyd Wright's appearance on What's My Line in September of 1956 shows he was quite a celebrity. Shortly after that, he was 88 in 1957 when he spoke with Mike Wallace about controversial topics such as modern art, religion, politics, and his plans for a mile-high skyscraper.

In Mr. Wright's case, it seems his deviation from the ideal — his extraversion — worked in his favor.

So Long, Frank Lloyd Wright

Frank Lloyd Wright died in 1959, at the age of 91. He was so famous that Simon and Garfunkel wrote the song So Long, Frank Lloyd Wright about him in 1970, over ten years after his death and more than a hundred years after his birth.

Mr. Wright's legacy lives on, in numerous videos on youtube.com, in organizations such as the Frank Lloyd Wright Foundation and, of course in the buildings he left behind. As a sign of more modern times, there is even a Frank Lloyd Wright-inspired tiny house.

Frank Lloyd Wright demonstrates how someone can achieve extraordinary fame by being a bit different from the norm.

By being a bit more outgoing and decisive than many others in his field — and by opening himself to his feelings and emotions while working in a discipline that largely depends on logic and mathematics — Mr. Wright inspired a new generation of architects to recognize that nature and their own sense humanity are important to their work.
